## Overview

UN Open Source Week 2025 was held at United Nations Headquarters in New York from **16–20 June 2025**. The event was co-hosted by:

- United Nations Office for Digital and Emerging Technologies (UN-ODET)
- United Nations Office of Information and Communications Technology (UN-OICT)
- Germany
- Dominican Republic
- Nigeria

The event brought together:

- 1,000+ participants
- 75 countries
- 160+ speakers
- Nearly 100 sponsors and partners

Core themes included:

- Open Source Program Offices (OSPOs)
- Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)
- Open AI governance
- Open standards
- Digital sovereignty
- Public-interest technology
- Open collaboration for the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

# Key Themes

# 1. Open Source as Public Infrastructure

A recurring message throughout the conference was that open source is no longer a niche software development model.

Participants repeatedly framed open source as:

- Essential public infrastructure
- A governance model
- A foundation for interoperability
- A mechanism for transparency and accountability

Key quote:

> “Open source technologies are not just tools; they are building blocks for inclusive development, transparent governance, and shared innovation.”
>
> — Maurizio Massari, Permanent Representative of Italy to the United Nations

---

# 2. Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)

DPI emerged as one of the central concepts of the week.

The conference repeatedly referenced:

- Digital identity
- Payment systems
- Data exchange
- Open interoperability
- Reusable digital building blocks

Examples discussed included:

- India Stack
- Estonia’s digital government infrastructure
- Brazil’s Pix payments platform
- Kazakhstan’s Digital Family Card

## Important DPI Concepts

### Open by Default

Speakers emphasized that DPI should:

- use open standards
- avoid vendor lock-in
- support portability
- remain publicly accountable

### Safeguards by Design

Repeated emphasis was placed on:

- privacy
- human rights
- trust
- inclusion
- safety frameworks

The UN Universal Safeguards Framework was discussed as a practical governance layer.

---

# 3. OSPOs (Open Source Program Offices)

The “OSPOs for Good” track focused on how institutions operationalize open source internally.

Examples included:

- AWS
- Carnegie Mellon University
- DINUM (France)
- South Africa Presidency
- GitHub

## Core Functions of OSPOs

- Governance
- Compliance
- Community engagement
- Procurement guidance
- Open source strategy
- Policy alignment

A major insight was that successful OSPOs require:

- executive sponsorship
- cross-functional coordination
- cultural change
- long-term maintenance investment

---

# 4. Open Source AI

Several sessions focused on defining and governing open AI systems.

Topics included:

- Open Source AI Definition (OSAID)
- Digital Public Goods standards
- transparency
- governance
- training data access
- compute infrastructure
- safety practices

Key concern:
“Open” must extend beyond source code.

Speakers argued that openness should include:

- governance
- datasets
- documentation
- evaluation methodologies
- accountability mechanisms

---

# 5. Maintenance as Infrastructure

The Maintain-A-Thon was one of the strongest signals that the conference recognized sustainability problems in open source ecosystems.

Topics included:

- maintainer burnout
- security
- funding
- contributor onboarding
- lifecycle governance
- software stewardship

Key insight:

> Innovation without maintenance does not last.

This aligned closely with broader public-interest infrastructure thinking.

---

# 6. Open Standards and Interoperability

Several sessions stressed that:

- open standards are governance tools
- interoperability is political, not just technical
- governments need reusable systems
- public procurement should encourage reuse

Important references included:

- GovStack
- Software Heritage
- UNECE
- Mozilla Foundation
- Wikimedia Foundation

# Strategic Takeaways

## For Governments

- Treat open source as infrastructure, not procurement edge-cases
- Invest in OSPOs
- Require open standards
- Prioritize interoperability
- Avoid vendor lock-in

## For Civic Tech Organizations

- DPI is becoming a dominant framing mechanism
- Open source governance matters as much as code
- Maintenance and sustainability are now policy concerns
- AI governance discussions increasingly expect openness

## For Open Source Communities

- The UN is legitimizing open source as a diplomatic and governance issue
- Public-interest framing is accelerating
- Global South participation is expanding rapidly
- Open source is now deeply tied to digital sovereignty debates
